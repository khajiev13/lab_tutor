A GraphRAG-Enhanced Multi-Agent System for Dynamic Knowledge Synthesis: A Comprehensive Literature Review and Revised Research Plan
The Paradigm Shift: From Reactive Systems to Agentic Educational Ecosystems
The integration of artificial intelligence within educational frameworks has experienced an unprecedented acceleration, fundamentally transforming the pedagogical landscape. Bibliometric analyses of academic databases reveal an explosive growth in Artificial Intelligence in Education (AIEd) research, with publications surging significantly post-2020 and shifting fundamentally toward socio-technical integration by late 2024 and early 2025.1 The market trajectory mirrors this academic enthusiasm; the global AI in education market size, estimated at USD 5.88 billion in 2024, is projected to reach USD 32.27 billion by 2030, growing at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 31.2%.2 Furthermore, empirical data from the 2024-2025 academic year indicates that adoption has reached near-universal levels, with 85% of educators and 86% of students actively utilizing AI tools for research, content generation, and instructional planning.3
However, early applications of Large Language Models (LLMs) in higher education were predominantly characterized by static architectures and reactive dialogue systems. These conventional paradigms, while effective for basic content generation and generalized query response, inherently lack the persistent representation of learner knowledge required for principled, long-term pedagogical support.4 The literature identifies a critical limitation in these early systems: their inability to execute proactive, goal-oriented reasoning or to synthesize complex, multi-hop conceptual dependencies reliably over extended educational interactions.4 Traditional Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) frequently relied on rigid, rule-based mechanisms that hindered effective adaptation to heterogeneous learners and evolving real-world contexts.6
To transcend these limitations, the research frontier has firmly pivoted toward agentic workflows. Agentic Artificial Intelligence represents a novel class of autonomous systems capable of dynamic knowledge synthesis, contextual reasoning, and adaptive planning.7 The evolution of AI agents in education is presently categorized into four foundational technological paradigms: reflection, planning, tool use, and multi-agent collaboration.9 By orchestrating multiple specialized agents, these frameworks transform LLMs from passive instructional instruments into collaborative, goal-directed ecosystems capable of handling complex downstream tasks.11
Recent empirical deployments in higher education provide compelling evidence for this paradigm shift. For instance, an LLM-driven Instructor Agent deployed as the primary instructional tool in a graduate-level cloud computing course at the Indian Institute of Science demonstrated that the structured integration of conversational AI agents fosters reflective learning and sustains inquiry-driven dialogue.12 These systems transition students from broad conceptual exploration to deeper, focused inquiry by maintaining continuity across instructional modules.12
Despite these advancements, the efficacy of any agentic tutoring system is entirely contingent upon the quality and structure of its underlying knowledge base. A foundational challenge in higher education is the highly fragmented and unstructured nature of teaching materials—spanning slides, lecture transcripts, diverse textbook formats, and institutional documents.14 Before an AI tutor can guide a student through a complex curriculum, these scattered pedagogical artifacts must be unified into an interconnected, machine-readable format. Consequently, contemporary research has converged on the intersection of Knowledge Graphs (KGs) and Multi-Agent Systems (MAS). Establishing a trustworthy foundation for retrieval, personalized tutoring, and curriculum alignment necessitates an architecture that explicitly maps prerequisite chains, mitigates hallucinatory generation through verifiable grounding, and dynamically updates in response to both academic evolution and external job market demands.14



Structuring the Unstructured: Automated Knowledge Graph Construction
The conversion of raw educational documents into clean, reusable, and semantically searchable knowledge is a critical prerequisite for advanced AI applications. Traditional approaches to constructing knowledge graphs, which rely on the manual curation of entities and relationships by domain experts, are highly labor-intensive, prone to scalability bottlenecks, and rapidly become obsolete in dynamically evolving academic fields.16 Modern solutions leverage the profound natural language understanding of LLMs to automate the extraction of structured information from unstructured texts, enabling the rapid deployment of graph databases from legacy course materials.17
Recent frameworks facilitate highly sophisticated extraction methodologies. One prominent approach is the Extract-Define-Canonicalize (EDC) pipeline, which utilizes structured output constraints to automatically identify concepts, entities, and typed relationships directly from instructional materials.18 In educational contexts, relationships are rarely simple semantic links; they encompass complex pedagogical dependencies such as prerequisite, enables, contradicts, or exemplifies.14 The literature highlights various LLM-based automatic construction methodologies designed to alleviate time consumption while preserving structural fidelity. By processing course syllabi, lecture transcripts, and slide decks, these models generate nodes that exhibit high semantic similarity with source materials, enabling a more efficient semi-automated creation process that often surpasses human annotators in speed and breadth.17
Studies conducted across 2024 and 2025 demonstrate that integrating deep learning models into graph construction significantly enhances entity recognition and relational extraction. For instance, multi-step methodologies involving JSON extraction, subsequent RDF/XML conversion, and iterative meta-learning have been successfully deployed to refine structural coherence in complex scientific and educational graphs.16 In these systems, iterative LLM prompting addresses persistent challenges such as mislabeled relationships and the generation of parasitic (irrelevant or disconnected) nodes.16
Furthermore, automated concept normalization pipelines are increasingly vital for maintaining the long-term health of educational knowledge graphs. These pipelines employ LLM-based reflection agents to intelligently merge synonymous terminology (e.g., recognizing that "Artificial Neural Networks," "ANNs," and "Neural Nets" refer to the identical conceptual entity) while rigorously preserving the distinctness of unique concepts.14 This iterative enhancement process progressively builds and refines the knowledge network, capturing intricate relationships without accumulating duplicate nodes over successive academic terms.14
A notable and highly impactful advancement in the 2025-2026 research timeframe is the shift toward Multimodal Knowledge Graph Construction (MMKGC). Educational materials are rarely purely textual; they inherently encompass complex diagrams, statistical charts, and mathematical equations. Conventional text-only extraction pipelines fail to capture the holistic pedagogical value of these resources.25 Emerging representation learning methods now employ multimodal embeddings with contrastive objectives to align heterogeneous modalities—such as images, video frames, and text—into coherent semantic spaces.25 By utilizing Vision-Language Models (VLMs) alongside traditional LLMs, modern pipelines automatically extract concepts from both textual descriptions and visual artifacts, achieving entity-image linkage without the need for manual annotation.25 This multimodal linkage forms a self-improving, virtuous cycle between knowledge building and subsequent reasoning, cementing the knowledge graph as a comprehensive, multi-dimensional digital twin of the curriculum.

Graph-Grounded Retrieval-Augmented Generation (GraphRAG)
While standard Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems have demonstrated substantial utility in mitigating LLM hallucinations by grounding responses in external documents, their reliance on pure semantic similarity limits their effectiveness in educational contexts. Standard RAG operates by measuring the vector distance between a user's query and chunked document embeddings.27 This methodology excels at retrieving discrete, contextually relevant passages for simple factual inquiries.29 However, complex educational interactions frequently require an understanding of hierarchical and prerequisite conceptual relationships.28 Naive RAG systems manage document complexity by dividing texts into smaller chunks for vector indexing; this approach inadvertently fractures crucial contextual information, significantly compromising the retrieval accuracy and contextual comprehension required for multi-hop reasoning.27
In response to these structural deficiencies, Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation (GraphRAG) has emerged as a superior paradigm.32 By incorporating explicit topological structures—derived from the automatically constructed educational knowledge graphs—into the retrieval pipeline, GraphRAG grounds generative models in verified, interconnected subgraphs rather than isolated text snippets.27 This approach fundamentally alters the retrieval mechanism: instead of merely searching for semantically similar words, the system traverses relational edges (e.g., navigating from a requested skill to its prerequisites, and subsequently to the associated teaching materials).32



Advanced implementations of GraphRAG have demonstrated profound capabilities. The Microsoft GraphRAG framework, for instance, utilizes hierarchical community-based search and global summarization of LLM-derived knowledge graphs to provide comprehensive responses with highly transparent evidence chains.20 By clustering related nodes into semantic communities, the system can answer high-level thematic questions that span an entire curriculum, a task where standard RAG typically fails.23 In the educational domain specifically, specialized adaptations like EDU-Graph RAG have been developed to automatically generate Knowledge Component (KC) structure graphs. These educational adaptations effectively capture both prerequisite and similarity relationships, serving as a critical computational foundation for recommending appropriate, personalized learning paths.20 Controlled experiments within intelligent tutoring environments reveal that grounding AI responses in these structured domain formats yields significant learning improvements, including marked increases in assessment scores compared to baseline RAG implementations.28
However, the deployment of GraphRAG is not without distinct engineering challenges. Empirical evaluations of page-level question answering using undergraduate textbooks indicate that while GraphRAG excels at modeling interconnected concepts, its entity-centric structure can occasionally lead to the retrieval of excessive and contextually noisy information.29 Extracting an entire subgraph can overwhelm the context window of the LLM, diluting the focus of the response. To address the degradation of performance caused by irrelevant retrievals and the suppression of the LLM's intrinsic reasoning capabilities, cutting-edge research has introduced hybrid filtering mechanisms. Frameworks such as GraphRAG-FI (Filtering & Integration) employ a rigorous two-stage filtering mechanism combined with a logits-based selection strategy.35 This sophisticated integration actively balances the injection of external structured knowledge with the model's internal cognitive pathways, reducing over-reliance on massive retrievals and establishing a significantly more reliable and effective GraphRAG ecosystem.35
Advancing Natural Language Interactivity via Text-to-Cypher and Text-to-GQL
The practical utility of an expansive, highly structured educational knowledge graph is entirely dependent on the system's capacity to execute complex queries against it seamlessly. While SQL has historically dominated relational database querying, the complex representation of educational curricula, prerequisite chains, market alignments, and multimedia resource mappings is optimally handled by graph databases such as Neo4j, NebulaGraph, or Amazon Neptune.36 Consequently, translating natural language directly into graph query languages—specifically the Text-to-Cypher and increasingly, the Text-to-GQL task—has become a highly active and critical research area for AI in education.36
LLMs demonstrate immense promise in semantic parsing for Text-to-Cypher generation; however, zero-shot and few-shot implementations relying solely on prompt engineering frequently suffer from syntax errors, logical misalignments, or schema hallucinations, rendering them unsuitable for reliable production environments.36 To bridge this critical translation gap, recent studies have concentrated heavily on fine-tuning foundational models utilizing dedicated, high-quality parallel corpora. Datasets such as CySpider, Rel2Graph, and SyntheT2C provide robust training grounds by mapping relational data to Cypher structures or generating synthetic training pairs.36 Furthermore, advanced architectural approaches leverage task splitting and sophisticated prompt engineering. Methodologies like Prompt2Cypher (P2C) decompose complex user queries into manageable subtasks. By handling entity recognition, relationship mapping, and syntax generation sequentially, P2C significantly enhances the LLM's ability to generate accurate queries that strictly align with the underlying database schema, demonstrating notable improvements in Precision, Recall, and Jaccard similarity metrics.42
The integration of agentic workflows has further refined Text-to-Cypher execution, pushing performance beyond what single-pass fine-tuning can achieve. Frameworks like Multi-Agent GraphRAG explicitly separate the generation process from execution and verification stages.43 In these architectures, one agent generates the initial Cypher query, while a distinct verification agent cross-references the proposed query against the explicit graph schema. If the query fails execution, the error log is fed back into a reflection agent for autonomous correction.43 By incorporating LLM-driven observation and database-grounded feedback, these multi-agent systems support robust, iterative query refinement, automatically correcting syntax or mapping errors before ever returning a flawed result to the user.43
The landscape of graph querying is also undergoing a standardization revolution. The release of the ISO GQL standard (ISO/IEC 39075:2024) in April 2024 has provided a unified, declarative query language for graph databases, heavily influenced by the syntax of Neo4j's Cypher.37 This standardization has prompted the development of comprehensive evaluation benchmarks like Text2GQL-Bench, which supports execution-based evaluation across multiple databases and abstract reasoning levels.39 The Text2GQL-Bench framework facilitates the scalable construction of datasets by converting heterogeneous data sources into executable graph databases, ensuring that Text-to-GQL systems can be rigorously evaluated for domain diversity and query complexity.39 In an educational context, achieving highly reliable Text-to-Cypher or Text-to-GQL translation is transformative: it allows instructors, administrators, and students to effortlessly interrogate the curriculum graph—identifying prerequisite gaps, querying specific skill coverage, or locating optimal study materials—without requiring any specialized database engineering expertise.14
Bridging the Curriculum-Skill Gap through Multi-Agent Labor Market Analysis
A persistent and escalating challenge in modern higher education is the rapidly evolving discrepancy between academic curricula and the tangible, practical skills demanded by the contemporary job market. As artificial intelligence and widespread automation reshape professional profiles across sectors, occupational expectations are shifting at a velocity that traditional, static university curricula struggle to match.44 The traditional model of multi-year curriculum review cycles is inherently ill-equipped to respond to the sudden emergence of new technological competencies.
Recent large-scale natural language processing analyses of university course descriptions versus industrial job advertisements reveal a stark alignment gap. A comprehensive study comparing leading UK university communications curricula against synchronous graduate-level job advertisements utilized a three-stage NLP workflow, including comparative TF-IDF salience and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling.44 The findings were highly illustrative of the broader systemic issue: academic curricula tend to foreground conceptual-critical themes—allocating significant vocabulary to ethics, platform governance, and societal impact (6.0% of total vocabulary)—while industry recruitment discourse focuses almost exclusively on operational execution, specific software toolchains, multichannel analytics, and campaign performance (allocating only 2.3% to broader conceptual terms).44 This divergence suggests that while universities excel at teaching foundational theory, they frequently overlook the immediate operational skills employers prioritize.
To bridge this curriculum-skill gap proactively, advanced AI-powered frameworks are being deployed to dynamically align educational offerings with external workforce realities. By leveraging the orchestration capabilities of multi-agent frameworks, institutions can implement autonomous swarms that scrape, analyze, and synthesize labor market data in real-time.47 In modern architectural proposals, this alignment is achieved through a coordinated sequence of highly specialized agents.14
Simultaneously with internal course analysis, a Market Demand Analyst agent operates as a collaborative swarm. It autonomously scrapes real-time job postings from major employment platforms using parallel processing APIs.14 Utilizing deep learning-based NLP and Large Language Models, the system extracts concrete, actionable skills from the unstructured text of job descriptions.50 Crucially, unlike monolithic or keyword-matching approaches that treat jobs as singular units, sophisticated pipelines decompose each job posting into constituent tasks. They categorize the importance of each extracted skill (Primary, Secondary, Ancillary) and employ synonym canonicalization to standardize varying industry terminologies into unified conceptual nodes within the knowledge graph.14
Once the labor market competencies are structured, a subordinate Curriculum Mapper agent autonomously compares these extracted external skills against the internal academic skills already present in the course knowledge graph.14 This comparative process accurately classifies every market-demanded skill as already covered, partially covered (identifying specific gaps), or entirely missing from the syllabus.14

Multi-Agent Orchestration for Resource Curation and Learning Personalization
Following the precise identification of the skills required to bridge the curriculum-market gap, the next imperative is the curation of appropriate pedagogical materials to teach those skills effectively. The curation process, when executed manually across dozens of rapidly evolving topics, is prohibitively time-consuming for instructional faculty. To automate this, contemporary educational platforms utilize advanced multi-agent orchestration frameworks, such as LangGraph and LangChain, which provide the infrastructural foundation for autonomous AI agents to collaborate, delegate tasks, and optimize curatorial workflows.48
Within a comprehensive GraphRAG-enhanced system, this curation is managed by dedicated curatorial swarms. First, a Curricular Alignment Architect agent searches vast repositories for candidate textbooks. It utilizes a ReAct (Reasoning and Acting) loop to evaluate materials against weighted pedagogical criteria, including topic alignment, pedagogical structure, and currency.14 Following instructor validation—a critical Human-in-the-Loop checkpoint—the agent segments the texts and extracts concrete, teachable skills directly into the knowledge graph, establishing the academic baseline.14
Subsequently, a Textual Resource Analyst and a supplementary Video Agent address the specific skill gaps identified by the market analysis. These agents utilize the newly unified skill vocabulary to execute highly contextualized web searches across multiple search engines and academic databases simultaneously.14 By evaluating the retrieved tutorials, official documentation, and multimedia content on criteria such as recency, pedagogical depth, clarity, and source authority, the system filters out low-quality content.14 The agents then automatically bind the highest-ranking external resources to the corresponding skill nodes in the knowledge graph, seamlessly connecting the abstract curriculum gap with tangible, immediately accessible learning materials.14
The ultimate realization of this structured, market-aligned educational knowledge graph is its application in next-generation Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS). Traditional ITS architectures, and even early LLM-based chatbots, were fundamentally reactive; they treated each user query in isolation, lacking the persistent awareness of a learner's evolving knowledge state necessary for long-term pedagogical support.5 The contemporary literature indicates a profound shift toward goal-oriented, multi-agent tutoring frameworks that operate over a centralized learner model.
Frameworks such as IntelliCode represent state-of-the-art implementations in this domain. Rather than a single chatbot, these systems utilize a StateGraph Orchestrator to coordinate a suite of specialized agents—including skill assessors, learner profilers, graduated hinters, and spaced-repetition monitors—over a shared, unified memory architecture.5 Operating under a strict single-writer policy, this architecture enables auditable mastery updates, proficiency-aware hinting, and dependency-aware curriculum adaptation.5 The tutoring swarm maintains a persistent, high-fidelity model of the student's cognitive progress, misconceptions, and engagement signals, dynamically adjusting learning pathways based on real-time feedback.4
Furthermore, educational theorists emphasize that AI tutors must foster critical thinking rather than simply dispensing answers. Advanced Socratic multi-agent architectures explicitly prevent "answer leakage." In these systems, a supervisory loop continuously monitors the interaction, utilizing the established knowledge graph to offer grounded hints while forcing the student to explain, justify, and revise their ideas.61 By strictly separating external generative knowledge from the curriculum-specific evidence stored in the graph, these systems ensure that formative feedback is highly scalable and rigorously aligned with predefined academic milestones.61 To manage the significant computational overhead of continuous multi-agent deliberation, novel memory architectures like D-Mem (Dual-Process Memory) utilize Quality Gating mechanisms. These gates dynamically route student queries between rapid associative recall for simple factual questions and exhaustive full deliberation for complex reasoning tasks, optimizing inference latency and token consumption while preserving pedagogical excellence.63
Ethical Safeguards, Privacy, and Cognitive Implications
While the transition to agentic, GraphRAG-enhanced educational ecosystems offers immense pedagogical benefits, it concurrently introduces significant ethical, privacy, and systemic challenges that must be proactively managed.65 Foremost among these is the persistent risk of hallucination and interpretive drift. Although grounding LLMs in structured knowledge graphs mitigates the generation of factually incorrect information, complex multi-hop reasoning tasks can still produce logically flawed inferences if the underlying ontology is ambiguous or if the retrieval mechanism surfaces noisy subgraphs.33 Consequently, the implementation of robust, domain-specific guardrail models is imperative to monitor agent outputs in real-time, preventing the dissemination of misleading educational content.67
Data privacy and security represent another critical vector of concern. Centralized learner models, which persistently track student misconceptions, engagement metrics, and behavioral patterns, accumulate vast amounts of sensitive personal information.5 The integration of these granular profiles within multi-agent systems necessitates the deployment of stringent access controls, anonymization protocols, and verifiable assurance mechanisms to comply with global data protection regulations and ensure that student data is not misappropriated for unauthorized algorithmic training.51 Furthermore, as these systems rely on large-scale data ingestion from public repositories and labor market platforms, considerations regarding algorithmic bias and fairness must be addressed to ensure that personalized learning pathways do not inadvertently reinforce demographic or socioeconomic disparities.51
From a cognitive perspective, researchers caution against the detrimental effects of technological over-reliance. When AI tutors are excessively accommodating or fail to enforce sufficient cognitive friction, students may default to utilizing the systems as shortcuts, thereby undermining the development of self-regulation, analytical reasoning, and critical thinking skills.69 To counteract this, pedagogical designs must prioritize "desirable difficulties" by employing Socratic questioning agents that prompt student reflection rather than immediate resolution.61 Ultimately, the literature converges on the necessity of hybrid human-AI workflows. Frameworks that integrate explicit instructor-in-the-loop checkpoints—where human educators moderate LLM outputs, validate curriculum alignments, and oversee agent behavior—consistently outperform fully autonomous systems by successfully marrying the scalable automation of artificial intelligence with the nuanced, empathetic expertise of human pedagogy.15
Modified Research Plan: A GraphRAG-Enhanced Multi-Agent Architecture (2025–2026)
The original research proposal 14 outlines a 9-month schedule (September 2025 – June 2026) aimed at constructing a course knowledge graph and executing an agent-driven curriculum enrichment pipeline. Based on the exhaustive literature review of advancements from 2024 to 2026, the foundational plan requires strategic modifications to incorporate state-of-the-art methodologies. The integration of multimodal extraction, hybrid GraphRAG filtering, advanced Text-to-GQL translation, and centralized learner state modeling will substantially elevate the project's technical efficacy and academic impact.
The following details the revised, phased research protocol:
Phase 1: Advanced Multimodal Knowledge Graph Construction (Months 1-2)
The original plan focuses heavily on textual extraction from slides and textbooks. The revised protocol integrates Multimodal Knowledge Graph Construction (MMKGC) to capture the full spectrum of educational data.25
Implementation: Deploy parallel pipelines utilizing both LLMs for textual analysis and Vision-Language Models (VLMs) for visual artifacts. Extract entities, mathematical equations, and systemic diagrams from course materials to achieve direct entity-image linkage in the Neo4j database.25
Refinement: Implement the Extract-Define-Canonicalize (EDC) framework to enforce strict ontology alignment. Execute iterative LLM-based reflection for synonym deduplication, ensuring a pristine, logically sound topological foundation.18
Phase 2: Agentic Curriculum Alignment and Market Mapping (Months 3-4)
The core operations of the Curricular Alignment Architect and Market Demand Analyst remain central but will be enhanced by modern graph-based orchestration and deeper semantic analysis.
Implementation: Utilize LangGraph to structure the agent swarm, facilitating asynchronous job scraping, robust error handling, and parallel textbook evaluation via ReAct loops.14
Enhancement: Incorporate advanced TF-IDF salience and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling algorithms. This approach will quantify the semantic gap between the extracted academic concepts and industrial job postings, moving beyond simple keyword matching to identify deeper thematic and operational alignments.44
Phase 3: Text-to-GQL Translation and Hybrid GraphRAG Implementation (Months 5-6)
To enable seamless interaction with the constructed graph, the system must translate natural language reliably and retrieve information without hallucination or context window overload.
Implementation: Develop a highly robust Text-to-Cypher translation layer. Instead of relying on zero-shot LLM queries, implement a Prompt2Cypher task-splitting protocol that decomposes complex academic queries into executable sub-queries, drastically reducing schema hallucinations.41
Validation: Benchmark the query engine against the newly established Text2GQL-Bench standards to ensure high schema fidelity, evaluating the system across diverse question abstraction levels.39
Retrieval Upgrades: Upgrade the standard retrieval module to a Hybrid GraphRAG-FI (Filtering & Integration) architecture. This mechanism applies logits-based selection to balance the retrieval of external graph structures with the LLM's internal reasoning, filtering out noisy nodes and preventing cognitive overload during generation.35
Phase 4: Development of the Centralized Learner State Orchestrator (Months 7-8)
To lay the true groundwork for future tutoring applications, the system requires a persistent, dynamically updating memory architecture rather than a simple reactive interface.
Implementation: Design a StateGraph Orchestrator modeled closely on the IntelliCode framework. Establish a unified, versioned learner state database that continuously tracks mastery estimates, review schedules, and skill deficiencies as students interact with the curated textual and video resources.5
Optimization: Implement Quality Gating (Dual-Process Memory) to dynamically route queries. Simple factual questions will trigger rapid associative recall, while complex pedagogical inquiries will route to exhaustive full deliberation, ensuring the multi-agent system remains highly responsive and cost-efficient.63
Phase 5: Human-in-the-Loop Validation, System Evaluation, and Dissemination (Month 9)
The final phase focuses on rigorous academic and practical validation to ensure systemic reliability and pedagogical value.
Implementation: Execute extensive instructor reviews across the textbook alignment, market extraction, and resource curation pipelines, ensuring all automated mapping decisions are verified by domain experts.14
Evaluation: Assess the complete end-to-end pipeline using both computational metrics (e.g., BLEU, Jaccard similarity, and BERT scores for GraphRAG accuracy) and qualitative pedagogical evaluation frameworks.73 Verify the system's capacity to identify actionable curriculum-skill gaps accurately and prepare the findings for open-source release and academic dissemination.
