# A Knowledge Graph-Centric Multi-Agent Framework for Curriculum-Market Alignment and Learning Resource Construction

## Abstract

Higher education courses increasingly face three linked challenges: teaching knowledge is fragmented across heterogeneous course materials, curricula evolve more slowly than labor-market skill demands, and the manual construction of supporting resources imposes substantial burden on instructors. To address these issues, we propose Lab Tutor, a knowledge-graph-centric multi-agent framework for intelligent curriculum resource construction and curriculum-market alignment. The framework organizes teacher-provided materials into a course knowledge graph, enriches that graph through textbook discovery and chapter-level skill extraction, aligns curriculum content with market-demand skills derived from job postings, and links identified skills to downstream learning resources under human-in-the-loop review checkpoints. We evaluate the framework through a Big Data course case study using two complementary analyses built on the same four curriculum variants: `KG`, `KG + B_S`, `KG + J_S`, and `KG + B_S + J_S`. In the course-level held-out benchmark, the strongest variant (`KG + J_S`) improved demand-weighted skill coverage from `0.2961` to `0.4806` and average job-fit from `0.2775` to `0.4602` across `8` held-out jobs drawn from `28` strict-scope Big Data roles. In a student-centered spillover analysis, skills extracted from `7` student-selected target job postings improved average job-fit on the other `55` jobs from `0.2588` to `0.4259`, while the fully personalized variant reached `0.4333`. These findings suggest that a knowledge-graph-centric multi-agent workflow can improve estimated curriculum-to-market alignment and can also help explain how skills learned for a small target job portfolio transfer to adjacent opportunities, while preserving instructor oversight at critical decision points.

## Keywords

knowledge graphs; multi-agent systems; curriculum-market alignment; learning resource construction; higher education

## 1. Introduction

Higher education is under growing pressure to show that university learning still translates into timely, degree-relevant employment. Aggregate employment indicators remain relatively strong in some regions, but recent-graduate outcomes still show clear mismatch and fragility. Eurostat reports an employment rate of 86.7% for recent tertiary graduates in the European Union in 2024, while U.S. evidence shows 5.7% unemployment and 42.5% underemployment for recent college graduates in 2025:Q4 (Eurostat, 2025; Federal Reserve Bank of New York, 2025). Together, these signals suggest that the challenge is no longer only access to higher education, but also the quality and timeliness of the alignment it produces.

The labor market is also changing faster than conventional curriculum revision cycles. Employers expect 39% of workers' core skills to change by 2030, and artificial intelligence, big data, and cybersecurity remain among the fastest-growing capability areas (World Economic Forum, 2025). When course revision depends mainly on periodic review, retrospective feedback, or local intuition, a curriculum can drift out of sync with external skill demand during the lifetime of a single student cohort. In practice, this means that curriculum quality must be judged not only by internal coherence, but also by how visible, diagnosable, and updateable its skill coverage is.

This pressure exposes three linked problems. First, teaching knowledge is fragmented across lecture notes, transcripts, slides, books, and other files that are hard to search and compare semantically. Second, curriculum-market alignment is weak because course revision often relies on lagging signals rather than continuously updated labor evidence. Third, teaching-resource construction remains labor-intensive: instructors must identify books, decide which additional skills deserve coverage, and curate supporting resources manually while the skill landscape continues to move. These problems are tightly coupled, which is why they should not be treated as separate workflow inconveniences.

To address them, we propose Lab Tutor, a knowledge-graph-centric multi-agent framework for intelligent curriculum resource construction. Lab Tutor uses a course knowledge graph as the shared semantic substrate for organizing teacher-provided materials, aligning them with textbook and labor-market skills, and linking identified skills to downstream learning resources. The workflow is organized into a knowledge foundation layer, a knowledge alignment layer, and a resource application layer, with human review checkpoints at critical decisions such as textbook approval, skill curation, and concept review (UNESCO, 2023).

We evaluate the framework through a Big Data course case study. Starting from course-document skills, we compare four curriculum variants: `KG`, `KG + B_S`, `KG + J_S`, and `KG + B_S + J_S`. The evaluation is framed as a curriculum-to-market alignment study rather than an employment-outcome study, and it includes both a course-level held-out benchmark and a student-centered spillover analysis. The central research question is: how can a knowledge-graph-centric multi-agent system organize fragmented course knowledge, align it with textbooks and labor-market skills, and support resource construction under human oversight? This paper contributes a graph-centered multi-agent framework, a unified transcript-to-book-to-market-to-resource alignment pipeline, and a case study showing both improved held-out curriculum-to-market alignment and broader opportunity spillover from a student's `7` target job postings.

## 2. Related Work

This section reviews the four research directions most relevant to Lab Tutor: educational knowledge graphs, AI-supported and multi-agent educational systems, curriculum-labor-market alignment, and educational recommendation. These directions are necessary because the paper does not contribute only a graph, only an alignment method, or only a recommendation workflow. Instead, it combines all of them into one teacher-centered pipeline. The purpose of this section is therefore twofold: to show which ideas are already well established in prior work and to clarify which combination of problems remains insufficiently addressed.

Research on knowledge graphs in education provides an important foundation for our work because it addresses the problem of fragmented educational content through structured semantic representation. Recent review literature argues that knowledge graphs are increasingly used in education for concept organization, learning support, curriculum analysis, and recommendation tasks (Abu-Salih & Alotaibi, 2024). System-oriented studies such as educational knowledge graphs linked to Wikipedia and course-centered graph systems show that heterogeneous educational artifacts can be normalized into entities and relations suitable for retrieval, analytics, and downstream applications (Dang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024). These studies support the core premise that curriculum knowledge should not remain trapped inside disconnected files. At the same time, most prior educational knowledge-graph systems focus on graph construction, concept linking, or recommendation in isolation. They do not typically combine course-graph construction with textbook alignment, labor-market alignment, and instructor-governed resource construction inside one end-to-end workflow. This distinction matters because our framework is not only building an educational graph; it is using that graph as the coordination layer across multiple curriculum decisions that would otherwise remain separate.

The second relevant line of work concerns AI-supported and multi-agent educational systems. Earlier research in conversational intelligent tutoring systems argued that multiple agents are useful when educational tasks require distinct roles such as explanation, reflection, assessment, and dialogue management. More recently, the broader literature on large language model (LLM)-based multi-agent systems has provided a taxonomy for role decomposition, coordination, and controllability in complex AI workflows (Guo et al., 2024). Education-specific work on LLM-assisted educational knowledge graph creation further shows that generative models can support course modeling and graph augmentation when outputs remain auditable and human-validated (Jhajj et al., 2024). This literature supports the architectural logic behind using specialized agents rather than one monolithic model. However, most existing systems focus on tutoring, grading, or graph construction in isolation. They do not directly target curriculum-market alignment or the construction of skill-linked educational resource pipelines.

The third literature group focuses on curriculum-labor-market alignment. Here, recent work has made curriculum analysis more computable by linking course artifacts to explicit skills, job activities, and labor-market taxonomies. Course-Skill Atlas provides a large-scale dataset for inferring skills from course syllabi, while a PLOS ONE study connecting higher education content to workplace activities and earnings shows that curriculum artifacts can be mapped to external labor ontologies in a systematic way (Javadian Sabet et al., 2024; Chau et al., 2023). Work in educational data mining has also modeled curriculum profiles against job-market needs in data-intensive fields, demonstrating that the curriculum-job gap can be studied as an empirical comparison rather than only a qualitative concern (Gromov et al., 2020). This literature is especially close to our problem setting. Still, it usually emphasizes large-scale measurement, static curriculum analysis, or taxonomy linkage rather than a teacher-facing system that both diagnoses alignment gaps and connects them to concrete enrichment and resource-construction actions. Our framework therefore builds on this literature while shifting the emphasis from retrospective measurement toward curriculum intervention and resource support.

The fourth relevant area is educational recommendation and personalized support. Educational recommender systems have a substantial research history, and recent reviews emphasize both their practical value and their dependence on high-quality learner, content, and contextual representations (da Silva et al., 2023). More specialized work on ontology-based and semantic recommendation in e-learning argues that structured knowledge representations improve contextual relevance, resource matching, and learning-path generation (Rahayu et al., 2022). This literature aligns with our resource application layer, particularly the idea that recommendations are stronger when they are grounded in concepts and skills rather than in isolated keywords. However, most recommendation systems start from learner-resource interaction data or personalization logic after the educational content structure is already assumed. Our framework starts earlier by constructing and aligning the course graph itself before downstream resource support is produced.

In summary, prior work establishes that each ingredient of our framework is individually plausible: educational knowledge graphs can structure learning content, multi-agent workflows can decompose complex AI tasks, curriculum-market alignment can be studied through skills and labor evidence, semantic recommenders can support resource selection, and human oversight remains essential in educational AI (Abu-Salih & Alotaibi, 2024; Guo et al., 2024; UNESCO, 2023; Javadian Sabet et al., 2024; da Silva et al., 2023). However, the literature still leaves a practical and methodological gap. Existing work rarely combines all of these elements inside a single teacher-centered pipeline that begins with fragmented course materials, aligns them with textbook and market evidence, and then connects the result to actionable resource support under instructor review. Lab Tutor is positioned to address that gap, which is precisely why the present paper moves from related work into an integrated methodology and case study.

## 3. Methodology

This section explains the methodology behind Lab Tutor and why the framework is divided into distinct parts rather than treated as one monolithic workflow. The architecture is intentionally separated because the underlying educational problems are different in nature. Fragmented course materials must first be structured into a stable internal representation; external evidence from textbooks and labor markets must then be aligned against that representation; and only after those two problems are addressed can the system support resource construction in a traceable way. Organizing the framework into these parts improves interpretability, supports human review, and makes the later evaluation more meaningful because each layer solves a specific problem and can be examined separately.

### 3.1 Framework Overview and Design Principles

Lab Tutor is designed as a knowledge-graph-centric multi-agent framework because the curriculum problem is layered rather than singular. Teacher materials must first be converted into a stable internal representation, external textbook and labor-market evidence must then be aligned against that representation, and only after those steps can the system construct downstream learning support without losing provenance or teacher control. This is why the workflow is decomposed into several coordinated agents instead of one monolithic pipeline.

Two design principles follow from this architecture. First, Neo4j is the shared semantic substrate rather than a passive storage layer. Chapters, concepts, book skills, market skills, job postings, and resources are connected through explicit relations, which makes later enrichment traceable and keeps the workflow anchored in the teacher-owned course scaffold. Second, the system uses progressive enrichment under human governance. The baseline graph is created first, textbook and market layers are added next, and downstream resource support is generated only after those layers are available. This staged design keeps the four-variant evaluation interpretable and preserves instructor authority at textbook approval, market-skill curation, concept review, and optional resource review (UNESCO, 2023).

### 3.2 Task 1: Document Extraction, Concept Structuring, and Embeddings

The first task constructs the transcript-grounded course knowledge base. Teacher-uploaded materials are parsed and passed through structured extraction to identify document topics, summaries, keywords, and explicit concepts. Each extracted concept carries semantic provenance, including a normalized name, a definition-like description, and textual evidence from the source material. The extracted information is written into the graph as teacher-uploaded documents and concept mentions, creating a traceable record of which concepts originate from which course artifacts.

This stage is important for two reasons. First, it converts passive instructional files into a queryable curriculum memory. Second, it establishes the anchor against which later textbook and market skills are aligned. After extraction, embeddings are generated for both documents and concept mentions, creating a semantic retrieval substrate that later workflows can use for comparison, search, and evidence grounding. In practical terms, the output of Task 1 is not yet a market-aware curriculum; it is the teacher-owned baseline representation of what the course currently teaches.

This distinction between baseline representation and later enrichment is central to the evaluation design. If textbook and market information were fused into the curriculum from the start, it would be difficult to tell whether later improvements came from the graph representation itself or from added external evidence. By maintaining a transcript-derived baseline, Lab Tutor supports ablation across curriculum variants and makes it possible to compare how different enrichment sources change the coverage of held-out market requirements.

### 3.3 Task 2: Textbook Discovery and Curriculum Alignment

The second task is implemented by the Curricular Alignment Architect, which builds the book-side skill bank. The workflow begins by generating a diverse set of textbook-oriented search queries from the course context, then searching external sources in parallel to identify candidate books. Candidate books are deduplicated before scoring so that different listings of the same title do not distort ranking. The retained candidates are then evaluated through a multi-criteria process over topic coverage, structural alignment, scope and depth, publisher reputation, author authority, and recency.

`S_{book}=0.30C_{topic}+0.20C_{struc}+0.15C_{scope}+0.15C_{pub}+0.10C_{auth}+0.10C_{time}`

The implementation also supports an optional practicality blend `S'_{book}=(1-W_{prac})S_{book}+W_{prac}C_{prac}`, where `C_{prac}` captures the presence of labs, code examples, and exercises; however, the default workflow keeps `W_{prac}=0`, so practicality is retained as an explicit criterion without changing the ranking score unless configured otherwise. The workflow then pauses for teacher review so that only instructor-approved books continue into the extraction pipeline.

After book selection, the system resolves PDF acquisition, extracts chapter structure, and performs chapter-level analysis. The important distinction here is that raw PDF extraction and chunking are not themselves the final book-skill output. The book-side skill vocabulary is created in the agentic chapter-extraction stage, where individual chapters are analyzed in parallel to produce chapter summaries, practical skills, and prerequisite concepts. These outputs are written into the graph as `BOOK_SKILL` nodes and `REQUIRES_CONCEPT` relations. A separate mapping flow then aligns extracted `BOOK_SKILL` nodes to the teacher-created course chapter scaffold. This step is what makes textbook-derived skills curriculum-relevant rather than isolated book artifacts.

Methodologically, textbook alignment contributes a form of disciplinary enrichment. Unlike market signals, which emphasize current employer demand, textbooks contribute structured pedagogical coverage, chapter-level organization, and prerequisite coherence. For that reason, textbook-derived skills should not be interpreted simply as another source of “more skills.” They serve a different purpose: they deepen and organize the curriculum representation from within the discipline, which is why they remain useful even when the measured lift is smaller than that of market-derived skills in the case study. In practical curriculum terms, this layer helps answer whether the course is internally coherent and instructionally supportable, even before the market-facing question of whether it tracks external demand closely enough. ### 3.4 Task 3: Market-Demand Alignment

The third task is the Market Demand Analyst, which builds the market-side skill bank and serves as the main evaluated layer in the case study. The current implementation uses a coordinated multi-agent workflow centered on supervisory orchestration, curriculum mapping, and concept linking, together with parallel skill extraction. The process begins with job discovery from relevant search terms and external job platforms. Job postings are deduplicated and grouped so that the instructor can retain only roles genuinely relevant to the target curriculum. This human filtering step is important because curriculum alignment is meaningful only if the selected jobs correspond to the course domain rather than to a broad or noisy market sample.

Once a relevant job subset is selected, job descriptions are processed in parallel to extract concrete market skills. The resulting skills are aggregated, cleaned, frequency-scored, and mapped against the curriculum graph. For each candidate skill, the workflow determines whether the skill is already covered, partially covered, or better treated as a new topic need. Later stages remove redundancies relative to existing curriculum content and link retained skills to prerequisite concepts before inserting them as `MARKET_SKILL` nodes in the graph. This stage turns labor-market evidence into curriculum-relevant diagnostic output rather than a raw list of job terms. It also reduces a common problem in curriculum-market studies: noisy demand vocabulary. Raw postings often contain platform boilerplate, duplicated requirements, company-specific terminology, and weakly relevant skill mentions. By forcing extraction, normalization, deduplication, and chapter-aware mapping before insertion, the workflow narrows the distance between market language and curriculum language.

The output of this task supports two complementary uses. In teacher-facing analysis, it exposes where a curriculum appears strong, weak, or silent relative to selected job evidence. In the graph itself, it creates a reusable market-side skill bank whose relations to chapters, concepts, and job postings can support later recommendation and analysis tasks. This dual role helps explain why the market-demand workflow is central both to the framework's educational value and to the empirical evaluation in this paper. ### 3.5 Task 4: Resource Curation and Student Learning Support

The fourth task connects aligned skills to downstream support resources. In Lab Tutor, this is implemented through textual and video-oriented resource discovery workflows that build context from the graph. For each selected skill, the system constructs a skill profile using related concepts, chapter context, and course level, then generates targeted queries for readings and videos. The retrieval stage already applies modality-specific eligibility constraints: reading results exclude social platforms, shopping pages, generic video sites, paywalled resources, and homework-mill style domains, while the video workflow restricts retrieval to YouTube-based educational content. The surviving candidates are passed through semantic filtering and then ranked through a shared multi-criteria scoring function that combines recency, concept coverage, embedding alignment, pedagogy, depth, and a modality-specific quality factor.

`S_{res}=0.15C_{rec}+0.25C_{cov}+0.15C_{emb}+0.20C_{ped}+0.15C_{depth}+0.10C_{extra}`

Here, `C_{extra}` corresponds to source quality for readings and production quality for videos. Before final selection, the implementation also applies an embedding-based prefilter that keeps the highest-alignment candidates for detailed LLM scoring. After scoring, the pipeline applies a greedy coverage-maximizing selector with `k=3`, preferring resources that jointly cover the largest set of skill concepts while also enforcing at least two resource types when possible. This means the final reading and video recommendations are not chosen only by individual score, but by score-and-coverage tradeoff over the concept set attached to each selected skill.

This layer must be described carefully. In the current implementation, readings and videos are primarily generated through the student learning-path pipeline rather than through a fully mature teacher-side planning interface. For the purposes of this paper, the resource application layer is therefore presented as an implemented downstream use of the aligned skill banks, not as a claim that all teacher-facing resource workflows are already complete. This wording keeps the methodological account faithful to the current system while still showing how the framework closes the loop from “what should be learned” to “how it may be learned.”

This boundary is important because it keeps the paper from overclaiming maturity in the application layer. At the same time, the resource workflows still matter conceptually. They show that the alignment graph is not an endpoint used only for diagnostics. Instead, it can act as the semantic bridge from curriculum analysis to practical support for learning. In that sense, the resource application layer is a downstream consequence of the graph and skill-bank design rather than an independent module.

### 3.6 Human-AI Review and Quality Assurance

Human review is a core methodological commitment of the framework rather than an auxiliary feature. In the textbook workflow, teachers review ranked candidate books before adoption and can intervene when automatic retrieval yields weak results. In the market-demand workflow, teachers constrain job relevance, curate skill sets, and determine which skills should be retained for insertion. Concept-level review is also preserved where merge or linkage decisions may affect curriculum interpretation. Optional review can further be applied to downstream resource recommendations before they are treated as finalized support materials.

This design aligns with current educational AI governance guidance, which argues that trust, interpretability, and accountability are especially important in teaching and curriculum applications (UNESCO, 2023). Methodologically, these review points also reduce the risk that the final system output reflects only model fluency rather than curricular judgment. They also preserve a realistic division of labor: the system can accelerate retrieval, extraction, and comparison, but instructors retain authority over what counts as pedagogically appropriate curriculum change.

### 3.8 Big Data Case Study and Evaluation Design

We evaluate the framework through a Big Data course case study and frame the evaluation as two complementary analyses. The baseline curriculum representation, `KG`, is built from course skills extracted from teacher-uploaded documents and organized by chapter. We then construct three enriched variants: `KG + B_S`, which adds textbook-derived skills; `KG + J_S`, which adds market-derived skills; and `KG + B_S + J_S`, which combines both enrichment sources. This four-variant setup functions as an ablation-style comparison that lets us observe the relative contribution of different enrichment paths at both the course and student levels. Table 1 summarizes the main case-study dataset and split configuration.

[Table 1 about here: Big Data case study dataset summary.]

The current artifact operates over `62` deduplicated job postings in the broad graph pool. For the course-level benchmark, the notebook applies a strict Big Data / Data Engineering relevance filter, yielding `28` main-benchmark jobs. These are split into `20` build jobs and `8` held-out jobs using a deterministic seeded procedure. For the personalized case study, the broad pool is retained: `7` job postings explicitly selected by `STUDENT(id=2)` form the seed portfolio, and the remaining `55` jobs are used to measure spillover beyond the student's original targets. In other words, the student case study asks whether the skills learned for `7` target jobs can also support many other adjacent jobs.

The evaluation uses persisted job descriptions for the current run, so job skills are extracted from raw job text rather than from graph-linked proxy skills. Each extracted skill is judged as `covered`, `partial`, or `missing` relative to a curriculum variant, and these labels are mapped to numeric values of `1.0`, `0.5`, and `0.0`, respectively. From these judgments we compute demand-weighted skill coverage and average job-fit score, with threshold-based success rates used as supporting indicators. The course-level benchmark remains a small held-out case study, whereas the student analysis is a personalized scenario in which the union of skills extracted from the student's `7` selected jobs is applied to the other `55` jobs in the graph.

The case study is designed to answer three questions. First, to what extent does the course-only curriculum cover the skills required by relevant Big Data job postings? Second, how much do textbook-derived and market-derived enrichment improve held-out curriculum-to-market alignment? Third, if a student prepares around `7` explicit target job postings, to what extent do the extracted seed-job skills improve fit for additional jobs outside the original target set?

### 3.9 Alignment Metrics

We report two primary metrics and one secondary threshold indicator. For the course-level table, let `J` denote the held-out job set and let `S_j` denote the set of extracted job-skill requirements associated with held-out job `j`. For the student table, the same scoring procedure is applied to the remaining jobs outside the student's seed portfolio. For each curriculum variant `v` and each evaluated skill instance `s`, the structured evaluator returns a judgment `y(v, s)` in `{1.0, 0.5, 0.0}`, corresponding to `covered`, `partial`, and `missing`.

Demand-weighted skill coverage measures how much of the evaluated market-skill demand is supported by a curriculum variant:

`Coverage(v)=\frac{\sum_s w_s\, y(v,s)}{\sum_s w_s}`

where `w_s` denotes the demand weight of skill instance `s` in the graph-backed evaluation. This metric is appropriate as the main headline score because it aggregates alignment at the skill-instance level while preserving the intuition that more salient demand signals should matter more than rare ones.

Average job-fit score is computed in two stages. First, skill-instance scores are averaged within each held-out job:

`JobFit_j(v)=\frac{1}{|S_j|}\sum_{s \in S_j} y(v,s)`

These job-level scores are then averaged across all held-out jobs:

`AvgJobFit(v)=\frac{1}{|J|}\sum_{j \in J} JobFit_j(v)`

This second metric is easier to interpret from a curriculum-planning perspective because it captures how well a curriculum variant performs on a typical held-out job rather than on the skill pool as a whole. As a secondary indicator, we also report threshold-based success rates at 0.60 and 0.80 when useful, but these are treated as supportive rather than headline metrics because the held-out sample is small and the current artifact is better suited to relative comparison than to pass-fail benchmarking.

In summary, the methodology progresses from internal curriculum structuring to external evidence alignment and finally to downstream educational support. The layered design is not only an architectural preference; it is a problem-solving strategy. The knowledge foundation layer addresses fragmentation, the alignment layer addresses curriculum-market and curriculum-textbook mismatch, and the resource layer addresses the practical burden of turning alignment findings into usable support materials. Together, these methodological choices make it possible to diagnose curriculum gaps, enrich the curriculum with interpretable evidence, and evaluate the resulting variants in a controlled case study.

## 4. Results

This section reports what the methodology produces in the Big Data case study. It first compares curriculum variants quantitatively at the course level, then turns to the student-centered spillover analysis, and finally discusses the practical meaning and limitations of the observed improvements. The aim is not only to report scores, but also to show what kind of curriculum problem the framework is actually able to make visible and partially solve.

### 4.1 Course-Level Held-Out Alignment

The course-level held-out benchmark shows a consistent pattern: curriculum enrichment improves estimated alignment relative to the course-only baseline, and market-skill enrichment explains most of the measurable lift. The baseline `KG` variant achieved a demand-weighted skill coverage of `0.2961` and an average job-fit score of `0.2775` across `8` held-out jobs. Adding textbook-derived skills alone raised coverage to `0.3398` and average job-fit to `0.3179`. Adding market-derived skills produced the strongest held-out result, increasing coverage to `0.4806` and average job-fit to `0.4602`, while also yielding the only `>= 0.80` success in the held-out set (`1/8`). The full variant, `KG + B_S + J_S`, remained far above the baseline at `0.4757` coverage and `0.4568` average job-fit, although in this particular split it did not surpass `KG + J_S`. Table 2 summarizes the course-level comparison directly.

These results support two immediate observations. First, course-document skills alone do not provide strong coverage of the evaluated external requirements. Second, textbook enrichment and market enrichment are not interchangeable. Textbook enrichment adds a modest but real gain relative to the baseline, whereas market enrichment produces the dominant lift because it injects missing external skill demand that is not strongly represented in the original course materials. The fact that `KG + J_S` slightly outperforms `KG + B_S + J_S` in this held-out split should be interpreted cautiously because the benchmark contains only `8` jobs and `KG + J_S` already injects the signal most directly matched to those held-out requirements. In this setting, the added book layer still contributes chapter-level breadth and academic structure, but some textbook-derived skills are overlapping or less hold-out-specific, so the combined variant can dip slightly without invalidating the value of textbook enrichment.

### 4.2 Student-Centered Opportunity Spillover

The personalized case study answers a different question: if a student prepares around `7` explicit target job postings, how far do those learned skills transfer to the rest of the job graph? Here the student baseline `KG` achieved a demand-weighted skill coverage of `0.2608` and an average job-fit score of `0.2588` across the `55` remaining jobs outside the seed portfolio. Adding the student's selected textbook skills produced only a small increase to `0.2671` coverage and `0.2646` average job-fit. This limited lift is consistent with the composition of the personalized book layer: the student has `25` selected book skills, but they are concentrated in concept-heavy Big Data, storage, and analysis chapters, so they deepen internal course structure more than they add new benchmark-facing engineering signals.

By contrast, the `7` seed postings are deliberately practice-oriented. They span `AI Development Architect`, `Data Architect`, two `Data Engineer` roles, `Full Stack Engineer`, `Engineering Intern`, and `Junior Developer`, and they yield `38` selected market skills that are much more skewed toward engineering work: architecture, cloud platforms, databases, APIs, pipelines, and implementation. That mix helps explain why spillover appears outside the original seed set: the stronger remaining-job matches include roles such as `Data Engineer` at Air Apps, `Enterprise Data Engineer Sr.` at First Horizon Bank, `Software Engineer, AI/ML, Workspace` at Google, `Software Engineer, Member AI Features` at SoFi, and `Senior Software Engineer, YouTube Ads ML` at YouTube. In those matches, the most reusable seed-job competencies are cross-functional data collaboration, robust data-pipeline development, cloud-native engineering, relational database work, and Python-based implementation.

The full personalized variant, `KG + B_S + J_S`, achieved the strongest overall student-case result at `0.4266` coverage and `0.4333` average job-fit. The difference between `KG + J_S` and `KG + B_S + J_S` is modest, which indicates that the dominant signal still comes from the `7` target-job skill portfolio. ### 4.3 Discussion and Limitations

From an educational perspective, the results suggest that alignment-aware curriculum enrichment can make hidden curriculum gaps more visible and more actionable. The transcript-only baseline reflects what the teacher-provided materials already express in the graph. The textbook-enriched variant adds disciplinary depth, chapter-level practical skills, and prerequisite structure. The market-enriched variant contributes the strongest observed lift in the course-level held-out benchmark because it directly injects labor-demand signals into the curriculum representation. In the student-centered table, the fully personalized variant performs best overall, which supports the idea that course design and student planning both benefit from combining internal academic structure with external demand evidence. Taken together, the two tables suggest that textbook enrichment mainly strengthens curricular coherence, whereas market and seed-job enrichment contribute the benchmark-facing engineering signal.

At the same time, the case study should be interpreted with appropriate caution. First, the evaluation is a focused Big Data case study rather than a multi-course benchmark. Second, the course-level held-out set contains only `8` jobs, so the benchmark remains small. Third, the student table is a personalized scenario analysis, not a held-out benchmark, because it intentionally asks how the skills from `7` target job postings can be applied to `55` other jobs. Fourth, the evaluation measures estimated curriculum-to-market alignment rather than real employment outcomes. It is therefore appropriate to claim improved visibility into skill gaps and improved estimated alignment, but not employability gains, hiring success, or causal labor-market impact on students.

These limitations also clarify the paper's future-work agenda. A stronger next step would be a larger held-out corpus with stored job descriptions, together with additional baselines and expert evaluation of the coverage judgments. Even with those limitations, however, the present case study is still useful: it shows that the framework can convert fragmented course materials, textbooks, and market evidence into a shared representation from which alignment differences across curriculum variants can be measured.

In this sense, the contribution of the case study is diagnostic rather than predictive. It shows that a curriculum graph can be enriched and compared against market-linked skill requirements in a way that exposes where the baseline curriculum is strong and where it remains incomplete. That diagnostic value is educationally significant even before the framework is validated at larger scale, because it gives instructors a structured basis for deciding what kinds of skills and resources deserve attention.

### 4.4 Implications for Curriculum Planning

The results suggest a practical interpretation of the framework for instructors and curriculum planners. The transcript-only graph can be viewed as a faithful representation of current course intent. Textbook enrichment then functions as a coherence layer: it surfaces prerequisite structure, chapter-level practice, and disciplinary breadth that may not be explicit in lecture transcripts alone. Market enrichment serves a different planning purpose. It highlights where the curriculum may need new examples, new tools, new case material, or even new chapter emphasis in order to speak more directly to contemporary external demand.

This division of labor matters for educational use. If market signals are treated as the only truth source, a curriculum risks becoming reactive and overly narrow. If textbook or internal course structure is treated as sufficient on its own, the curriculum may remain pedagogically coherent but drift away from current skill expectations. The current results support a more balanced position: a useful curriculum-planning workflow should preserve the teacher-owned conceptual scaffold, deepen it with textbook structure, and then selectively enrich it with external demand signals under human review.

The case study therefore supports Lab Tutor less as an auto-curriculum generator than as a structured curriculum intelligence system. Its most defensible contribution is helping instructors see what is already covered, what is weakly represented, and what may deserve targeted enrichment through readings, projects, examples, or supplementary learning resources. That type of visibility is valuable even in settings where institutions move slowly, because it turns curriculum revision from an opaque manual process into a more traceable and evidence-linked one. In summary, the results show measurable but still partial improvement: the framework does not solve curriculum-market mismatch completely, yet it does make the mismatch more explicit, more measurable, and more actionable for teacher-guided revision.

## 5. Conclusion

This paper presented Lab Tutor, a knowledge-graph-centric multi-agent framework for intelligent curriculum resource construction and curriculum-market alignment. The framework addresses three linked problems in higher education: fragmented course knowledge, slow response to labor-market change, and the high manual cost of building and updating instructional support materials. By organizing teacher-provided materials into a course knowledge graph, enriching that graph with textbook and market-derived skills, and linking aligned skills to downstream learning resources under instructor review, the framework provides a structured pathway from curriculum representation to actionable curriculum support.

The Big Data case study shows that curriculum enrichment improves estimated curriculum-to-market alignment relative to a course-only baseline. In the current course-level held-out evaluation, market-derived skills contribute most of the observed lift, while textbook-derived skills provide additional but smaller gains. The personalized case study adds a second perspective: the student's `7` target postings span architecture, data engineering, full-stack, and junior software roles, and the extracted seed-job skills substantially improve fit for many other jobs in the graph. In practice, that transfer is carried by overlapping competencies such as SQL and data profiling, pipeline engineering, Python-based implementation, cloud-native tooling, APIs and microservices, and testing or automation. These findings do not demonstrate employment outcomes, but they do show that a knowledge-graph-centric multi-agent workflow can expose and reduce curriculum-market skill gaps in a measurable way while also illustrating how a student's chosen target jobs can support broader opportunity spillover. Just as importantly, the case study suggests that curriculum intelligence is most useful when it remains teacher-centered: the graph makes hidden gaps visible, the enrichment layers make external demand comparable to internal course structure, and human review keeps curricular decisions accountable to educational judgment rather than model fluency alone.

Future work should strengthen the evidence base through larger held-out evaluations, richer job-text persistence, and broader multi-course validation. It should also continue improving the resource application layer so that alignment diagnostics and learning support become more tightly connected in practical educational use. In that sense, the present contribution is not a claim that curriculum-market mismatch has been solved once and for all. Rather, it is a demonstration that a knowledge-graph-centric multi-agent workflow can turn fragmented course materials, textbook evidence, and labor-market signals into a common, reviewable representation from which curriculum gaps can be analyzed more systematically and acted upon more responsibly.
